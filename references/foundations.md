# Pareto Startup Thinking Foundations

## Core Claim

The Pareto principle is useful in startups because impact is often uneven:

- a few features drive most usage
- a few problems drive most pain
- a few bottlenecks limit most progress
- a few decisions create most future complexity

But the right modern startup version is not:

- `apply the 80/20 rule mechanically`

It is:

- `find the vital few, verify them, and act on the real bottleneck`

## Why Pareto Alone Is Not Enough

On its own, Pareto thinking is too shallow.

It needs three companions:

- Theory of Constraints:
  - improve the system bottleneck first
- Lean Startup:
  - when uncertainty is high, learning speed matters
- YAGNI / simplicity:
  - complexity added too early becomes carry-cost

## PM-Friendly Heuristic

Use this filter:

1. What outcome are we trying to move?
2. What is limiting progress right now?
3. What are the likely drivers?
4. Which few are load-bearing?
5. What should we stop, defer, or simplify?
6. What is the smallest high-leverage next move?

## Misuse

This method fails when people:

- force fake precision
- ignore important but non-dominant work
- mistake visible work for leverage
- optimize local wins over system goals
- use `focus` as cover for underinvesting in quality or risk reduction

Good Pareto startup thinking is disciplined, not slogan-based.

## What Makes This Skill Worth Using

The edge is not `focus` as a slogan.

The edge is this combination:

- bottleneck-fit
- learning speed
- carry-cost awareness
- explicit kill/defer decisions

That makes it more useful than a simple priority list when startup resources are tight.
